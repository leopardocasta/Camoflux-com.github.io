Notes from the install: Incendio Igapó at the Whitney

On staging a playable game inside a museum, fiberglass furniture as scenography, and the awkward beauty of strangers watching strangers play.

DEVLOG_INCENDIO-IGAPO-WHITNEY_HERO.JPG

Installing Camoflux at the Whitney has been an exercise in translation. A game is normally a private encounter — one player, one screen, one set of headphones. The Biennial install asks the work to become public: a screen on a wall, fiberglass seating arranged around it, a vinyl decal on the floor, and people who came for sculpture suddenly negotiating a controller.

What surprised me most is how the seating choreographs attention. We tried three configurations before landing on the current one. When the chairs face the screen too directly, the room feels like a movie theater — wrong. When they're scattered, no one sits down. The current arc, slightly tilted inward, lets visitors observe each other observing the game. The work is partly about that triangulation.

The fiberglass pieces are deliberately unfamiliar. Not gallery benches, not gaming chairs — something closer to organic furniture from a world that hasn't quite happened. They cue visitors that the rules of the room are different.

We open March 8. If you're in New York between then and August 23, come by. The work is on Floor 6, near the Connolly piece. The controllers are on a 90-second cooldown so everyone gets a turn.
